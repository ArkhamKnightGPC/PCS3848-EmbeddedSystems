\section{Context}

This monograph documents a project proposal submitted for the course PCS3848: Embedded Systems at the Escola Politécnica da Universidade de São Paulo (EPUSP). It contains the specification for an embedded AI system for real-time driver state monitoring and alerting, to be implemented in the Embedded Systems Laboratory course.

\section{Objectives}

%\begin{itemize}
%    \item Apresentar o objetivo do trabalho de forma precisa e concisa.
%    \item O que é o trabalho?
%\end{itemize}

We propose a real-time driver state monitoring system for cars and trucks.

The primary objective of this system is to help drivers, specially when they are alone in the vehicle for prolonged periods, by alerting and preventing them from driving in case they are speeding, under the effect of alcohol or other drugs, not wearing their seat-belt, distracted or sleepy.

All these traffic infractions are factors that contribute to potentially serious accidents, especially for truck drivers. The idea is to use simple computer vision models trained with specialized platforms such as TensorFlow Lite to capture images of the driver and, in the event an undesired state is detected, emit an audible alert and provide appropriate recommendations to assist the driver and engage their attention.

Observe that the key metrics to be minimized here are \textbf{latency per model inference} and \textbf{accuracy} so the drive can react sufficiently fast in the vast majority of risky scenarios. Unlike some embedded systems applications, power consumption is not critical here because the system can be powered via USB using the car's battery during its operation.

The development of driver monitoring systems is an active field of research \cite{review} involving many different types of sensors: cameras, microphones, radar, biometric sensors and even exploiting mechanical on the vehicle. There is a critical trade-off between sensing accuracy and the computational constraints of embedded hardware. While non-visual methods like radar offer privacy benefits, camera-based systems remain the most versatile for capturing nuanced behavioral indicators. For this reason, we choose to focus on the camera-based visual approach.

The most relevant works consulted are summarized below:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item \cite{essa2026} benchmarks seven different lightweight deep learning architectures for real-time embedded driver state detection. The benchmark uses Kaggle's State Farm Distracted Driver Detection multimodal classification dataset to evaluate and compare the model architectures. The architectures considered achieve accuracies of over 90\% with very small footprints, sometimes smaller than one megabyte.

    \item \cite{lumina2026} presents a robust embedded solution implemented on NVIDIA's Jetson Orion Nano GPU using the YOLOv11 nano (YOLOv11n) model architecture for real-time drowsiness monitoring in drivers. The methodology focuses on high-speed object detection to monitor facial traits in the driver.The quantities monitored include  Eye Aspect Ratio (EAR) and Mouth Aspect Ratio (MAR) to identify states of drowsiness or distraction. The system achieves a low latency per inference of approximately 22ms with n overall accuracy of 93.1\%.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Commercial Driver Monitoring Systems}

After our bibliographical review, we started looking for material on driver monitoring systems from companies to see what commercial products already exist or are being developed for the market. We soon realised that the field of Driver Monitoring Systems (DMS) is characterized by a significant divide between high-end, proprietary solutions and research-oriented embedded systems.

In the United States and Europe, there is movement to popularize DMS in a wide range of vehicles and legislation is likely to impose DMS soon, specially trucks where they could prevent most accidents. Startups like \textbf{Seeing Machines}, \textbf{Novus Hi-Tech} and \textbf{Mobileye} develop commercial DMS for big automotive companies like indian manufacturer \textbf{Eicher Motors}, \textbf{Tesla} and \textbf{Stellantis} with robust performance, but they often require expensive, specialized hardware and are integrated deeply into a vehicle's proprietary architecture.

In contrast, the embedded system architectures we saw in our bibliographical study are designed as a generic, modular, low-cost alternative that maintains competitive performance through optimized TinyML architectures. Our project, AIware, fits in this research trend. Instead of developing a system integrated with a manufacturer's proprietary technology, the goal of our system is to allow implantation on any vehicle after manufacture is finished.
